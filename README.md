## Research Overview
El Niño (/ɛl ˈniːnjoʊ/, /-ˈniːn-/; Spanish: [el ˈniɲo]) is the warm phase of a recurring climate pattern across the tropical Pacific Ocean characterized by prolonged periods of abnormally high sea surface temperatures coupled with the weak and eastward-displaced atmospheric circulation of the Walker circulation. The Southern Oscillation, a more dynamically complex phenomenon, describes the seesaw pattern of reversing surface air pressure between the eastern tropical Pacific and the western Pacific in the tropical atmosphere, and El Niño is the most prominent, irregular, climatologically important component of the Southern Oscillation. In normal years, winds blow east to west across the surface of the tropical Pacific Ocean, pushing warm surface water toward the western Pacific where it dominates the sea surface temperature (SST) pattern in that area. During El Niño events, the winds weaken and the warm water shifts eastward, resulting in cooler-than-normal SSTs in the western Pacific and warmer-than-normal SSTs in the eastern tropical Pacific. The mechanism driving this phenomenon is a positive feedback loop between the ocean and the atmosphere. The warming of the eastern Pacific suppresses the upwelling of cold, nutrient-rich deep water, leading to further warming. This shift in the tropical Pacific Ocean temperature pattern affects global atmospheric circulation, leading to weather changes worldwide, including droughts in some regions and heavy rainfall and flooding in others.
